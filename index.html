01
De Keuken Groep (DKG)
Truck Loading Optimization
PythonStreamlitDocker
+

Replacing manual truck loading decisions for one of the Benelux's largest kitchen manufacturers with an intelligent 3D bin packing system.

Kitchen cabinets present unique logistics challenges — they're bulky, fragile, and must be loaded in precise sequences to enable efficient unloading at multiple delivery stops. DKG was experiencing suboptimal truck utilisation (around 60%) due to manual loading decisions, resulting in increased transportation costs and safety risks.

I designed a 3D bin packing system using a DBLF (Deep Bottom-Left Fill) heuristic that enforces real-world constraints: LIFO (Last In First Out) requirements for multi-stop deliveries, Dutch ARBO law for safe weight distribution, and zero violations for fragile items. The system processes DKG's dataset of over 1 million items across thousands of transport orders.

The final deliverable is a Dockerised Streamlit application that covers the entire workflow in one pipeline — data cleaning, 3D loading optimisation, checklist generation, and printable sticker output — designed to be used directly by non-technical warehouse staff.

Project Highlights
  • Built a DBLF heuristic enforcing LIFO compliance, Dutch ARBO law, and zero fragile item violations across 1M+ items
  • Complete data cleaning pipeline: dimension consolidation, unit error correction, missing value imputation via Random Forest Regressor
  • Interactive 3D Plotly visualisation of loading sequences — usable by non-technical warehouse staff
  • Dockerised Streamlit app delivering data cleaning, truck loading, checklist, and sticker generation in one pipeline
Key Takeaways
  • Translating complex logistics constraints (LIFO, fragility, weight distribution, rotation limits) into algorithmic rules requires deep domain understanding, not just technical skill
  • Classical heuristics often outperform ML approaches for highly constrained optimisation problems
  • Delivering professional documentation and printable outputs for warehouse staff is as important as the algorithm itself

ANWB
Road Safety Incident Prediction
Gradient BoostingSQLCNN
+

Predicting the type of driving incident a trip will produce — speeding, harsh braking, or cornering — using ANWB telematics data enriched with weather and road conditions.

ANWB provided driving behaviour data from real trips, but understanding what conditions lead to specific incident types required richer context. I merged this dataset with weather data and road condition datasets from a SQL data warehouse, giving the model environmental awareness alongside raw telemetry.

  • Deployed as a Streamlit stakeholder app and a FlutterFlow mobile client app

03
NPEC
Plant Root Segmentation & Robotic Control
U-NetPyTorchPPO / RL
+

A dual-track project for the Netherlands Plant Eco-phenotyping Centre: automating root measurement from images using computer vision, and training a robot to handle liquid inoculation using reinforcement learning.

NPEC researchers were manually measuring root lengths from Petri dish images — a time-consuming and inconsistent process. I built a U-Net segmentation pipeline that automatically identifies root structures and measures their length, reaching F1: 0.81 after iterating on BatchNorm layers, LeakyReLU activations, and learning rate scheduling.

In parallel, I designed and trained a PPO (Proximal Policy Optimisation) reinforcement learning agent in the PyBullet physics simulator. The agent was tasked with controlling a liquid-handling robot arm to perform inoculation — a physically precise task that requires the model to learn spatial reasoning and fine motor control entirely through reward signals.

Together, these two systems represent a complete automation pipeline for plant phenotyping: vision for measurement, and robotics for intervention.

Project Highlights
  • Built a U-Net segmentation pipeline reaching F1: 0.81 for automated root length measurement
  • Iterated on BatchNorm, LeakyReLU, and LR scheduling to improve segmentation quality
  • Trained a PPO reinforcement learning agent in PyBullet for robotic liquid-handling control
Key Takeaways
  • Iterative architectural improvements (BatchNorm, activation functions, scheduling) matter as much as model selection
  • RL agents in physics simulators require careful reward shaping — sparse rewards lead to very slow learning

04
NLP Pipeline
Arabic Emotion Classification
MARBERTWhisperAzure ML
+

An end-to-end NLP pipeline that classifies 7 emotional states from Arabic video content — transcribing speech, translating it, classifying emotion, and explaining model decisions, all the way to cloud deployment.

Arabic NLP presents unique challenges: the language is morphologically rich, dialectally diverse, and underrepresented in standard NLP toolchains. This project tackled the full pipeline from raw video to deployed model.

Whisper was used for high-quality Arabic speech transcription. A pretrained translation model converted Arabic text to English to enable cross-lingual transfer. The emotion classifier itself was MARBERT — a transformer model specifically pre-trained on Arabic text — fine-tuned on a dataset of 39,000+ labelled sentences across 7 emotion categories, achieving F1: 73.

Explainability was built in from the start: SHAP and LIME were applied to make model decisions interpretable, not just accurate. The entire system was then productionised — wrapped in a FastAPI backend, a CLI tool, a Streamlit interface, containerised with Docker, and deployed to Azure ML for scalable inference.

Project Highlights
  • Used Whisper for Arabic audio transcription and a pretrained model for English translation
  • Fine-tuned MARBERT on 39,000+ labelled sentences, achieving F1: 73 across 7 emotion classes
  • Applied XAI techniques (SHAP, LIME) for model interpretability
  • Full production stack: FastAPI, CLI, Streamlit, Docker, and Azure ML deployment
Key Takeaways
  • Arabic-specific pretrained models (MARBERT) dramatically outperform multilingual alternatives for this task
  • Explainability is not an add-on — building it in from the start changes how you design and evaluate the model
  • The gap between a working notebook and a production-ready system is where most of the real engineering happens

Deep Learning
AI Skin Disease Classifier
CNNGrad-CAMFairness AI
+

A CNN classifying benign vs malignant skin lesions — designed with explainability and skin tone fairness as core requirements from day one, not afterthoughts.

Medical AI carries real risk: a model that performs well on average but fails for certain skin tones can cause direct harm. I built this classifier with that concern at the centre of every design decision.

The dataset was self-curated from Kaggle and the web to ensure representation across skin tones. Grad-CAM was applied to visualise which regions of an image drove the classification — making predictions interpretable for users without ML backgrounds. LIME provided local explanations at the individual prediction level.

Skin tone bias was addressed explicitly using a Fairness Through Awareness approach, which conditions the model on protected attributes rather than ignoring them. The interface was designed in Figma and validated through think-aloud sessions and A/B testing with real users — not just evaluated on benchmark metrics.

Project Highlights
  • Self-curated dataset ensuring representation across skin tones for benign/malignant classification
  • Applied Grad-CAM and LIME to produce visual explanations interpretable for clinical users
  • Addressed skin tone bias using a Fairness Through Awareness approach
  • Designed a Figma prototype and validated UX through think-aloud sessions and A/B testing
Key Takeaways
  • Medical AI fairness requires proactive data curation — you cannot fix bias after the fact with post-processing alone
  • User testing with real people reveals usability issues that no benchmark metric can detect

NAC Breda
Football Player Market Value Prediction
Random ForestMySQLStreamlit
+

Analysed professional football players across 114 metrics to predict market value and optimal playing positions — built as a data-driven scouting tool for NAC Breda's analytics team.

Football clubs face a persistent challenge: placing players in the positions where they'll perform best. NAC Breda operates in a competitive league environment where every squad decision carries weight, and there's almost no room to trial a player in an unfamiliar role mid-season. This project builds the data infrastructure to answer that question before it goes on the pitch.

I developed a multi-class classification pipeline trained on European football player statistics, processing 114 features including physical attributes, passing metrics, defensive contributions, and attacking output. Multiple models were trained and compared — with Random Forest delivering the strongest classification results for position prediction. In parallel, a Ridge + TruncatedSVD regression pipeline achieved R² = 0.889 for predicting expected assists (xA per 90).

Beyond the models, I built a full MySQL database management system for persistent data storage, implemented gradient descent from scratch using NumPy to validate the regression approach, and delivered a Streamlit application allowing NAC Breda analysts to upload updated player data and immediately visualise team market value comparisons without writing code.

Ethical considerations were central to this project: I documented the class imbalance problem honestly (the 99.88% accuracy figures were an artefact of imbalanced classes, not genuine performance), applied GDPR analysis to the data pipeline, and proposed fairness auditing recommendations for ongoing model use.

Project Highlights
  • Ran 20 EDA questions on market value, xG, position performance, and contract duration across 114 features
  • Trained Linear Regression, Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, SVM, KMeans, and XGBoost
  • Achieved R² = 0.889 on xA per 90 prediction using a Ridge + TruncatedSVD pipeline
  • Built a MySQL database management system with create, query, and list operations
  • Built a Streamlit app for interactive team market value visualisation
  • Implemented gradient descent from scratch with NumPy to predict market value
Key Takeaways

  • Delivering a working tool (Streamlit app, MySQL system) matters as much as the model results for a real client
  • Ethical documentation — honestly reporting limitations — is part of professional data science work

Data Analysis
SDG GDP Growth & Emissions Analysis
Power BIPower QueryEDA
+

Does a country's economic growth inevitably come at the cost of its environment? This project investigates that question using World Bank data across Australia, Germany, and France — and the answer is more nuanced than a simple yes or no.

The research question driving this project was: to what extent does the economic development and growth of a country influence its carbon emissions and greenhouse gas levels? It is a question with real policy implications — if GDP growth and emissions are tightly coupled, then sustainable development requires fundamentally different economic models.

I sourced data from the World Bank and data.gov, collecting Annual CO2 emissions, Real GDP growth, Total GHG emissions, and pollution-related mortality across 2014–2019. The three countries were chosen deliberately: Australia is large with historically high emissions, Germany is an industrial economy in transition, and France represents a more decoupled growth model — together they tell different sides of the same story.

Data cleaning was done entirely in Power Query. This included trimming and filtering columns to focus only on relevant years and countries, converting the Entity column in Total-GHG-emissions from strings to numbers using a custom IF function column, and merging the real GDP growth and total GHG emissions tables to enable direct visual comparison on the same chart axes.

The final Power BI dashboard spans three pages. Page one tracks Real GDP growth by year using bar charts and a line chart with trend lines, with a correlation coefficient card. Page two overlays the Sum of Real GDP and Annual GHG emissions over time — revealing that while GDP fluctuates, GHG emissions remain relatively stable, suggesting partial decoupling. Page three focuses on the human cost: deaths from unsafe water and air pollution by country, showing that Australia has the lowest pollution-related death count while Germany has the highest, despite their GDP differences.

The headline finding: when the economy grows, emissions tend to rise, and pollution-related deaths correlate more strongly with air and water quality than with GDP itself, meaning that environmental health policy matters independently of economic growth.

Project Highlights
  • Sourced and merged datasets from World Bank and data.gov covering CO2, GHG, GDP, and pollution mortality (2014–2019)
  • Cleaned all datasets in Power Query: column trimming, type conversion, custom IF columns, and query merging
  • Built a 3-page interactive Power BI dashboard: GDP trends, GHG comparison, and pollution-related deaths
  • Calculated correlation coefficients between GDP growth and GHG emissions — overall coefficient: −0.01
  • Australia had the lowest GDP growth but also the lowest pollution-related deaths; Germany had the highest of both
  • GHG emissions remained relatively stable despite GDP fluctuations — indicating partial decoupling over the period
Key Takeaways
  • GDP and emissions are correlated but not as tightly as assumed — energy mix and environmental policy matter more than raw economic size
  • Pollution deaths are driven by air and water quality, not directly by GDP — this challenges the simple "growth equals harm" narrative
  • Dashboard design for a non-technical audience requires storytelling: each page should answer one question clearly
  • Missing data in early years made regression analysis impossible — data quality constraints must always be documented honestly
